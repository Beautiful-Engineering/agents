## The 4 Quality Criteria

Every piece of content must pass these four tests. Quality is what makes readers slow down instead of skimming.

### 1. Concision
Convey points without fluff. Cut the "writer's personality" and write clinically when needed.

**Test**: Can you remove a sentence without losing meaning? If yes, remove it.

**Bad**: Two paragraphs defining a term before answering the question the reader came for.
**Good**: Answer the query first. Provide context later if needed.

**Rule**: Every article should be organized to answer the searcher's query thoroughly and quickly.

### 2. Clarity
Points come across fully and seamlessly. The reader doesn't need to guess or re-read.

**Test**: Ask someone to read it. If they need to re-read any section, rewrite it.

**Bad**: Big words, abstract concepts, jargon, long complex sentences.
**Good**: Specific examples that illustrate the point. Simple language. Short sentences.

### 3. Depth
Go beyond what people already know — without going unnecessarily into the weeds.

**Test**: Would the reader learn something they didn't already know? Would they say "I didn't know that"?

**Bad**: Self-evident advice ("follow your passion").
**Good**: Counter-intuitive conclusions backed by data ("Job data says don't always follow your passion").

**Depth signals in titles**: counter-intuitive conclusions, reference to research/data, nuance ("don't always" instead of "don't").

### 4. Engagement
Hook readers in. Excite them about what they're learning.

**Test**: Would YOU keep reading this? Does the intro make you want to continue?

**Key**: Articulate what got you interested in the topic. That enthusiasm transfers.

## Harry Dry's 3 Rules of Good Copy

Apply these to every section you write:

1. **Can you visualize it?** — Concrete and specific beats abstract. "1,000 songs in your pocket" not "32GB storage capacity." "Worn by supermodels in London and dads in Ohio" not "Worn by everybody."

2. **Can you falsify it?** — Can the claim be proven true or false? "Quality you can trust" is meaningless. "Reads every day" is concrete. Point to facts and examples.

3. **Can nobody else say this?** — Is this unique to you/the product? Generic claims don't differentiate. Find what only you can say.

**If you have three no's, rewrite. If you have three yes's, you're on to something.**

## Visual Writing Guidelines

How you format the post affects how people read it.

### Images on their own line
Never wrap text around images. Place images on their own line — forces readers to look at them at exactly the right moment.

### Bold sparingly
Pick the 2-3 most important lines and bold those. No more. Over-bolding overwhelms.

### Only underline links
If something is important, bold it. Underlining = links only.

### Headers: larger, bolded, separated
Headers must be visually distinct from body text. Proper spacing above and below.

### Proper spacing
Space between paragraphs. Comfortable line height. No cramped text.

### Use bulleted and numbered lists
Lists create visual spacing, force shorter sentences, and are often used by Google as featured snippets.

### Table of contents
Link to each H2 section. Increases time-on-page. Can generate "jump to section" in Google results.

### Keyword in title
The target keyword must appear in the title, letter-for-letter, fully intact, and early.

## The 10 Hook Types

Choose one (or combine multiple) for the blog post intro. Match the hook to the topic and audience.

### 1. Credibility
Tell people WHY they should listen to YOU on this topic.
- Your experience: "I've grown 3 SaaS products from 0 to $1M ARR..."
- Your effort: "After analyzing 500 landing pages..."
- Someone else's credibility: Reference a respected person/company

### 2. Fear
Leverage loss aversion and negativity bias.
- FOMO: "If you're not doing X, you're falling behind..."
- FOBO (Fear of Being Outdone): Show someone achieving impressive results
- "I hope I'm not doing it wrong": Challenge a common practice
- "I hope that doesn't happen to me": Cautionary tale

### 3. Curiosity
Create an information gap the reader must fill.
- "Then what happened?" — Tease an intriguing story
- "I have to know more" — Bold claim without the explanation
- "What is it?" — Show something, tease the insight
- Open loops: "I don't know who needs to hear this..."

### 4. Counter-Narrative
Contradict what people believe. Spiky points of view.
- Challenge conventional wisdom with evidence
- "Everyone says X. They're wrong. Here's why."
- Works because strong opinions provoke thought and sharing

### 5. Eloquence
Put voice to an unvoiced concern, frustration, or opinion.
- "Finally, someone said it!" — Articulate what people feel but haven't expressed
- "That's so damn true" — Tap into shared frustrations
- Hardest to write — requires deep audience understanding

### 6. Faces (for visual content)
Use facial expressions in featured images/thumbnails.
- Shocked faces, selfies, celebrity/authority faces
- We're hardwired to notice faces displaying strong emotions

### 7. Value
Simply promise useful information. Tell them exactly what they'll get.
- "Here are the 7 strategies that took us from 0 to 10K users..."
- Handle objections upfront: "No fluff. No theory. Just what worked."
- Make sure you deliver on the promise (no clickbait)

### 8. Surprise
Lead with a surprising fact or statistic.
- "I didn't know that!" — Use unexpected data
- People love sharing surprising discoveries (high share rate)
- Statistics work best — concrete, falsifiable, quotable

### 9. Celebration
Celebrate a milestone or achievement.
- People feel compelled to engage (easy to say "congratulations")
- Works for company milestones, personal achievements, industry wins

### 10. Identity
Signal who the post is for so those people self-select.
- Barnum-style: Specific enough to feel personal, broad enough to apply widely
- Direct labeling: "Attention startup founders who..."
- Use terminology the audience uses to describe themselves

### Combining hooks
The best intros combine 2-3 hook types. Example: Credibility + Value + Surprise = "After analyzing 500 SaaS landing pages (Credibility), here are the 7 patterns that convert at 2x the average (Value) — and #3 will surprise you because it contradicts everything you've read (Surprise)."

## Engagement Techniques (Beyond the Hook)

### Conflict
Anything without conflict is boring. Introduce tension:
- X vs. Y comparisons
- "Here's the problem → here's why it sucks → here's the solution" (PAS)
- "Here's how they do it → here's how we do it"
- Tell a story of someone experiencing the problem

**Conflict makes the problem feel worse and the solution feel better.** Like pickle juice makes orange juice taste sweeter.

### Minimum Viable Backstory
When telling stories, include just enough context to set the stage. Cut everything else. Readers wait desperately for the point — don't make them wait.

### Make it about them
People are for themselves. Focus on what's in it for the reader, not your product features.

### Specificity
"1,000 songs in your pocket" beats "32GB storage." Specific details create trust and memorability.
