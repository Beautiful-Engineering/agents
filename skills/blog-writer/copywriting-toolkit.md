## 7 Copywriting Frameworks

Use these for blog post sections, CTAs, introductions, and persuasive passages. They're fill-in-the-blank templates built on psychological biases (loss aversion, herd mentality, framing effect).

### 1. PAS(P) — Problem, Agitation, Solution, Proof
Most commonly used. Great for blog intros and CTA sections.
- **Problem**: Identify audience pain point
- **Agitation**: Make the problem more intense/visceral
- **Solution**: Introduce your solution
- **Proof**: Evidence it works (testimonial, data, case study)

### 2. BAB — Before, After, Bridge
Paint the transformation. Good for product-focused sections.
- **Before**: Current bad situation
- **After**: How amazing life could be
- **Bridge**: How your product gets them there

### 3. AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
Good for blog conclusions and CTA sections.
- **Attention**: Bold headline or intriguing fact
- **Interest**: Build with a story or data
- **Desire**: Vivid picture of life after
- **Action**: Clear, compelling CTA

### 4. Storytelling (Pixar Framework)
Powerful for case studies and example sections.
- Once upon a time, ___
- Every day, ___
- Until one day, ___
- Because of that, ___
- Because of that, ___
- Until finally, ___

### 5. PASTOR — Problem, Amplify, Solution, Testimony, Offer, Response
Extended PAS. Good for long-form sections or email sequences.

### 6. The 4 P's — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push
All about visualization. Good for benefit-focused sections.
- **Promise**: Bold claim about what's possible
- **Picture**: Help them see their improved life
- **Proof**: Back it up with evidence
- **Push**: Reason to act now

### 7. PRUNE — Point, Reason, Unveil, Nail, Exit
Unique for its use of analogy to drive the point home.

## Rapid-Fire Copywriting Tips (Editing Checklist)

Apply these when reviewing each section of the draft:

1. **Make it about them** — Focus on what's in it for the reader. "People aren't against you, they're just for themselves."
2. **Make it relatable** — Use analogies the reader already understands. Ask: how do customers explain this to a friend?
3. **Cut the fluff** — Remove adverbs, adjectives, filler words. Restructure sentences to be punchier.
4. **Use simple words** — No $10 words when $0.05 words work. No jargon. Explain it to a 10-year-old.
5. **Be specific & visual** — Spell it out. Don't make them imagine. Show concrete details.
6. **Use active voice** — Shorter, sharper sentences. Subject does the action.
7. **Tell a story** — Illustrate with examples. Use Minimum Viable Backstory.
8. **Make it punchy** — Steal from poetry. Chop sentences. Add rhythm. Literary devices.
9. **Handle objections** — Identify what almost stopped them. Proactively address it. "What almost stopped you from buying?"
10. **Be bold** — Take strong stances. No wishy-washy statements. Find your tribe.
11. **Appeal to interest, not reason** — People are irrational. Appeal to selfish interests, not logic. Emotions drive action; logic rationalizes after.

## Benefit of the Benefit

When writing about product features or benefits, find the second-order benefit.

**Process**:
1. List all product benefits AND downsides
2. For each, ask: "What's an unexpected/obvious/funny/convenient benefit of this benefit/downside?"
3. The second-order benefit is often the real selling point

**Examples**:
- Trampoline → fun for kids → **parents get peace and quiet**
- Dog bed → dog has a place to sleep → **he won't sleep in your bed**
- Electric car → no gas stations → **don't care when gas prices spike**
- Sports car → no back seats (downside) → **no room for kids** (benefit for some)

**Application in blog posts**: When describing how something helps the reader, don't stop at the first benefit. Push to the second or third order. That's where the real resonance lives.

## Relatable Truths

Tap into shared experiences, frustrations, or observations that your audience immediately recognizes.

**Categories**:
- **Context**: Truths related to where/when the message is seen
- **Holidays/events**: Seasonal truths (New Year resolutions, tax season, back-to-school)
- **Industry**: Truths specific to the reader's profession or niche
- **Cultural**: Hype trends (AI, remote work), culture shifts, memes
- **Human**: Core human behavior and psychology (embarrassment, procrastination, social dynamics)

**Application**: Weave relatable truths into blog intros to create instant connection. "You know that feeling when..." or reference a shared frustration without naming it — let the reader nod along.

**Goal**: Make the reader think "Finally, someone said it!" or "That's so damn true."

## Using Conflict

Conflict is inherently interesting. Content without conflict is boring (nobody binges a textbook).

**How to introduce conflict in blog posts**:
- **X vs. Y** comparisons (including before/after)
- **PAS structure**: Problem → why it sucks → solution
- **"How they do it vs. how we do it"** — contrast approaches
- **Storytelling with a problem**: Someone experiences a frustrating situation, then resolves it

**The principle**: "Pickle juice makes orange juice taste sweeter." The contrast of extremes makes both more intense — the problem feels worse, the solution feels better.

**Application**: Don't just state facts. Frame them as a contrast. Don't say "Loom's positioning is good because X." Instead, show what happens without good positioning, then show the difference.

## Trigger Events

A trigger event is what pushes someone from "I know about this problem" to "I need to solve this NOW."

**Types**:
- **Seasonal**: New Year, tax season, back-to-school, industry conferences
- **Random/personal**: Got a new job, moved cities, had a bad experience
- **Social**: Friend bought it, competitor launched something, industry shift
- **Macro**: Recession, regulation change, technology breakthrough

**Application in blog posts**: Reference the trigger event in your intro or hook. If someone is reading your post about "how to choose a CRM," they probably just hit a trigger (outgrew their spreadsheet, lost a deal, new sales hire). Acknowledge that moment — it creates instant relevance.

**Finding trigger events**: Ask customers: "What was going on in your life when you decided to buy?" The stated reason is often not the real reason — dig deeper.

## Microwave Headlines (Quick Headline Generation)

When you need a good headline fast, use these 6 techniques:

1. **Say it straight** — State the facts plainly. Sometimes the factual version is already good. Forces clarity.

2. **Smile headlines (The Mullet)** — Business in front, party in back. Factual message + humor twist. "Follow me on LinkedIn. Or I'll keep following you in person."

3. **Common quotes/phrases** — Take a well-known phrase and twist it to fit your topic. "The pen is mightier than the sword" → "The prompt is mightier than the pen."

4. **Find opposites** — Pair contrasting words. "Small rates. Big deal." / "Fast as lightning. Quiet as a whisper." / "Search less. Find more."

5. **100 MPH Writing** — Set a 15-minute timer. Write as many headline ideas as possible. Judge later.

6. **Fill buckets** — List high-level value props → flesh out with specific scenarios → turn scenarios into headlines. "From sweaty to ready." / "Do burpees. Don't smell like burpees."
