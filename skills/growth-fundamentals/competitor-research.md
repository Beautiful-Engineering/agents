# Competitor Research

Competitor research isn't about staying ahead of rival companies — it's about extracting growth insights. Well-established competitors have tested dozens of iterations to find what works. You can get those learnings in a fraction of the time by studying their landing pages, ads, and content marketing.

**Key caveat**: Competitor research should never replace your own experimentation and customer research. The goal isn't to clone someone else's strategy — it's to understand at a macro level what worked for similar companies so you can better prioritize which channels and messaging have the most potential.

---

## Part 1: Identify Competitors

Use these four methods to build your competitor list:

### 1. Talk to leads and customers
Ask what other companies they've considered or used. If they name companies that aren't actual competitors, your brand positioning or value props may not be clear enough.

### 2. Search online directories
Use Crunchbase, Product Hunt, and G2. Search by topic/category and keep narrowing down to find both direct and indirect competitors.

### 3. Break down your user's workflow
Map out how your target audience handles the specific problem your product solves. Break the workflow into smaller tasks and identify which products complete each task. Products that appear most frequently or carry out the same tasks as yours are likely competitors.

### 4. Google your target keywords
See what websites rank highly for your target keywords. You'll find both direct and indirect competitors, plus roundup posts listing others.

---

## Part 2: Grade Competitors

Not all competitors are worth studying. Grade them on three criteria:

### Quality (Is the company successful enough to learn from?)
- **Revenue**: High revenue (or significant fundraising from reputable investors) speaks to growth success
- **Size**: Teams that have grown to 30-50+ people beyond founders tend to be more developed
- **Growth track record**: Long-term, sustainable growth provides more reliable insights than early-stage hype

The best companies to study hit all three. Note: quality matters less when defining value props — early-stage startups getting press or funding can be useful for messaging inspiration.

### Rigor (Is the company dedicated to growth experimentation?)
Look for at least two of these three indicators:
- **Public experiment results**: Blog posts about A/B tests, experiments, growth tactics
- **Growth roles**: LinkedIn shows team members or open positions for growth, experimentation, or CRO
- **Experimentation software**: Use BuiltWith to check for paid analytics and testing tools

### Relevance (How similar is their business to yours?)

**Classification hierarchy** (most to least relevant):

1. **Direct competitors** — Same problem, same customer, same solution (e.g., Uber vs. Lyft)
2. **Indirect: different solution** — Same problem and customer, different approach (e.g., gym membership vs. workout app). Most relevant indirect type — same consumer psychology.
3. **Indirect: different problem** — Same solution and customer, different problem (e.g., Uber vs. Uber Eats). Watch these — they may enter your space.
4. **Indirect: different customer** — Same problem and solution, different audience (e.g., Tinder vs. Raya). Usually least relevant — different market psychology.

## Part 3: Collect Growth Insights

Research competitors' landing pages, ad channels, and content marketing for growth insights.

### Landing Pages
Study how competitors describe their brand and products:
- **Problem**: What pain point do they focus on? How do they relate it to the user?
- **Features**: What features do they emphasize most?
- **Pricing**: How much do they charge? Different tiers? Any segments priced out?
- **Perks**: Bonuses that come with the product (early access, communities, etc.)

Focus on how they communicate value, not feature-by-feature comparison. What story do they tell? How do they position relative to others?

**A/B test detection**: Watch for URL parameters like `?optimizely_opt_out=true`. Open the same URL in incognito, refresh until the page changes, screenshot both variants. Reverse-engineer their hypothesis (new value props, different imagery, social proof formats).

### Ad Channels
- **Retargeting**: Browse competitor sites without an ad blocker, then watch for retargeting ads on social media. Note ads with the most comments/reactions.
- **Google Search Ads**: Use Ahrefs — enter competitor URL, go to Paid Search > PPC keywords. Also check Top Landing Pages to see how they segment by value prop and audience.
- **Meta & LinkedIn Ad Libraries**: Search [Meta Ad Library](https://www.facebook.com/ads/library/) and [LinkedIn Ad Library](https://www.linkedin.com/ad-library/home) for competitor ads.
- **Creative inspiration tools**: Foreplay and Atria for saving and analyzing curated ad creatives.

### Content Marketing
- **SEO**: Use Ahrefs or Semrush to find top organic keywords, traffic estimates per page, and most-linked content. Look for common themes across top-performing pages.
- **Brand awareness**: Use Google Trends to track branded searches over time. Steady growth suggests their tactics are working; decline is a cautionary tale.
- **Social media audit**: Answer three questions:
  1. Are competitors actively using social media? (Not just having an account — actively posting?)
  2. If active, how are they using it? Which platforms, what content types, influencer partnerships?
  3. How do users respond? Look at authentic comments (not vanity metrics like follower counts)

---

## Questions Sequence

1. Who are your known competitors? (Include companies your customers and leads have mentioned)
2. What other companies appear when you Google your target keywords?
3. Can you map your user's workflow — what tools/products do they use alongside or instead of yours?
4. For each competitor: how would you rate their quality (revenue/size/growth track record)?
5. For each competitor: what evidence do you see of growth experimentation rigor?
6. How would you classify each competitor — direct, or indirect by solution/problem/customer?
7. Based on grading, which 3-5 competitors are most worth studying in depth?
8. What initial observations have you noticed from competitors' landing pages, ads, or content?
