# Growth Catalysts

Growth Catalysts are structural advantages that create compounding growth. They are the X-factors — features of your business DNA that enable disproportionate growth relative to effort. There are two types: Flywheels (self-reinforcing loops) and Boosters (amplifiers).

The strongest growth comes from catalyst **combinations** — the effect is exponential, not additive.

---

## Flywheel Catalysts (4 types)

Flywheels are the gold standard. They create self-reinforcing loops where each cycle makes the next one stronger.

### 1. Network Effects
Value of product increases with number of users/partners.

**Three subtypes:**
- **Direct**: More users directly increase value for all users (Facebook, LinkedIn — more connections/content)
- **Two-sided**: More users on one side increase value for the other (Airbnb — more hosts = better for guests, more guests = better for hosts)
- **Data/Technology**: More usage generates more data that improves the product (Google, TikTok algorithms)

**Key test for data/tech network effects:** Having proprietary data doesn't equal a network effect. The data gains must directly improve the **core value** of the product.

### 2. Scale Flywheel
Increased volume drives down unit costs, enabling lower prices or higher investment, attracting more customers.

- Amazon: scale feeds directly into core value prop (lowest cost, most convenient)
- For most companies: creates a **margin advantage** that can be reinvested
- Most powerful use: enables higher CAC tolerance (outspend competitors on acquisition)

**Key caveat:** You generally need to hold the *greatest scale advantage* in your market. If your competitor has equal scale, net gain is limited.

### 3. Embedded Flywheel
Ongoing usage creates ever-growing user investment (data, customization, integrations, habits), making it harder to leave.

- Strongest in **B2B** where roots span multiple org functions/systems (Salesforce)
- **Weakest** flywheel type overall — switching has gotten easier, especially in consumer tech
- Leans heavily on retention side only (other flywheels amplify acquisition + monetization too)

### 4. Brand Flywheel
More customers either strengthen the core brand promise or chip away at a market constraint, attracting even more customers.

**Three patterns:**
- **Breaking social constructs**: More customers erode taboo → more openness → more customers (Hims, Thinx, Airbnb normalizing stranger stays)
- **Reinforcing associations**: More exposure deepens brand-promise association → less decision friction (Coke=happiness, Nike=athletic excellence)
- **Community as brand**: The brand's value *is* the community; more members strengthen it (CrossFit)

**Key test:** Each new customer must *reinforce or project the brand* in a way that compounds value. Simply having more customers doesn't create a brand flywheel.

**Timing note:** Association-type brand flywheels are long-term (decades). Taboo-breaking and community flywheels can create short-term breakout conditions — more accessible to startups.

## Booster Catalysts (6 categories)

Boosters amplify growth but are less defensible than flywheels. They provide temporary or conditional advantages.

### Market Boosters
- **Underserved market**: Blue ocean — targeting a neglected segment (low defensibility once others notice)
- **Market/regulatory shifts**: Early to spot a macro change (Deel + pandemic remote work shift)

### Product Boosters
- **User-generated assets**: Users create content/tools that attract other users (Notion templates ecosystem)
- **Built-in product virality**: Inherently social, emotional, or share-worthy product (not the same as network effects — virality = distribution, not compounding utility)

### Model Boosters
- **Free pricing**: When your cost structure allows free access while competitors can't afford to (Zoom's efficient architecture). Only a catalyst if competitors can't match it.

### Brand Boosters
- **Founder story**: Celebrity or high-credibility founder with built-in trust
- **Counter-positioning**: Positioning that incumbents can't copy without undermining their own business (Dollar Shave Club vs Gillette)
- **Category creation**: Inventing a new category and owning it (hardest to pull off, powerful if successful)

### Channel Boosters
- **Emerging channel timing**: New platform with low competition and favorable economics (window eventually closes)
- **Platform/ecosystem catalysts**: Third parties build on you, creating distribution (Shopify App Store, Slack App Directory)
- **Exclusive audience access**: Owning a large, targeted audience at low cost (newsletter, creator channels, community)

### Company Boosters
- **Elite founding team**: Unusually strong talent density
- **Privileged network access**: Connections to celebrities, investors, partners (OpenAI + Microsoft)
- **Capital**: Outsized access to funding

## Catalyst Identification Questions

Walk through in two passes:

### Pass 1: Current Catalysts
For each of the 6 categories (Market, Product, Model, Brand, Channel, Company):
- Do any catalysts exist in your business today?
- What evidence supports this?
- How strong is it relative to competitors?

### Pass 2: Future Catalysts
- Which catalysts could be intentionally built over time?
- What foundational changes would unlock them?
- What's the effort/timeline?

### Key Strategic Questions
1. Do you have any flywheel catalysts? (These are the priority — self-reinforcing)
2. Which boosters amplify your existing flywheels?
3. What catalyst combinations could create exponential effects?
4. Are any current boosters at risk of being neutralized by competitors?
