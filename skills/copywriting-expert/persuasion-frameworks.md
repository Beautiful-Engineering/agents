# Persuasion Frameworks

Core frameworks for structuring persuasive copy. Select based on copy type, audience awareness level, and product complexity.

Source: Sam Parr's Copy That curriculum — Days 2 (AIDA), 3 (Slippery Slope), 5 (Story-Sell), 9 (Yes Ladder), 10 (Advertorial/Hero's Journey)

---

## 1. AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action

The universal sales structure. Works for almost any copy type. From Gary Halbert's direct response tradition.

### How It Works

| Stage | Job | Techniques |
|-------|-----|-----------|
| **Attention** | Stop the scroll. Break the pattern. | Bold claim, surprising stat, provocative question, pattern interrupt, contradiction |
| **Interest** | Make them lean in. "Tell me more." | Story, data, social proof, curiosity gap, relatable truth |
| **Desire** | Make them WANT it. Feel the transformation. | Paint the after state, benefit-of-benefit, contrast with/without, social proof, scarcity |
| **Action** | Tell them exactly what to do next. | Clear CTA, remove friction, add urgency, reduce risk (guarantee) |

### Implementation Guide

**Attention (5-15% of copy length)**:
- Lead with the single most compelling thing about your product/offer
- The headline must earn the first sentence. The first sentence must earn the second.
- Test: Would someone stop scrolling for this? Would they read past the fold?

**Interest (20-30% of copy length)**:
- Build on the attention hook — don't pivot to something new
- Use minimum viable backstory — just enough context to understand the stakes
- Introduce the problem in vivid, specific terms
- This is where you earn the right to sell

**Desire (40-50% of copy length)**:
- This is the meat. Show the transformation.
- Use the benefit-of-benefit technique: don't stop at the first-order benefit
- Stack proof: testimonials, case studies, data, demos
- Handle the top 3 objections before the reader raises them
- Make the reader feel what life is like AFTER

**Action (5-10% of copy length)**:
- One CTA. Not three. One.
- State exactly what happens when they click/buy/sign up
- Remove risk (money-back guarantee, free trial, no credit card)
- Add urgency only if it's real (don't manufacture it)

### Best For
- Sales pages
- Product descriptions
- Email sequences (each email = mini AIDA)
- Ad copy (compressed AIDA)
- Landing pages

## 2. Slippery Slope

Each sentence's only job is to make you read the next one. The reader should feel like they're sliding down a slope — they can't stop reading.

From Joe Sugarman's axiom: "The sole purpose of the first sentence in an advertisement is to get you to read the second sentence." Demonstrated by Ben Franklin's autobiography technique and Joel Spolsky's engineering blog.

### Core Techniques

**Open Loops**
Start a story or question but don't resolve it immediately. The brain craves closure.
- "There's one thing every great sales page has in common. But most copywriters get it backwards."
- Don't close the loop until you've delivered the information you need them to absorb

**Curiosity Gaps**
Create a gap between what the reader knows and what they want to know.
- Tease a result without revealing how
- Reference something unexpected: "The email that made $2.3 billion"
- Use specificity to imply a story: "At 2am on a Tuesday, everything changed"

**Escalating Commitment**
Each paragraph asks for a slightly bigger commitment than the last.
- Start with something universally agreeable
- Build to your specific claim
- By the time you make the ask, they've already agreed to 10 smaller things

**Micro-Yeses**
Embed small agreements throughout. The reader nods along before you ask for the big yes.
- "You've probably noticed that..." (yes)
- "And when that happens, it's frustrating..." (yes)
- "What if there was a way to..." (tell me)

**Transition Bridges**
Connect paragraphs with bridges that pull the reader forward:
- "Here's why that matters..."
- "But that's only half the story."
- "Which brings us to the real question..."
- "And then something unexpected happened."

### The Exit Ramp Test

Read your copy paragraph by paragraph. After each one, ask: "Could the reader stop here and feel satisfied?" If yes, you have an exit ramp. Fix it by:
1. Opening a new loop before closing the previous one
2. Adding a transition bridge
3. Teasing what's coming next
4. Ending with a question or incomplete thought

### Best For
- Long-form sales pages
- Email sequences (each email ends with an open loop for the next)
- Founder letters
- Advertorials
- Any copy longer than 500 words

## 3. Story-Sell Format

A parallel story structure that creates identification before pivoting to the product. The reader sees themselves in the story, then discovers your product is the solution.

From the Wall Street Journal's "Two Young Men" letter — the most successful sales letter in history ($2 billion in subscriptions).

### Structure

1. **The Parallel** — Introduce two people (or two versions of the same person) in similar situations
2. **The Divergence** — Show how their paths split. One struggled, one thrived.
3. **The Identification** — The reader sees themselves in the struggling version
4. **The Pivot** — Reveal what made the difference (your product/service/approach)
5. **The Proof** — Show evidence that the thriving path is real and achievable
6. **The Close** — Invite the reader to take the thriving path

### Implementation Rules

- The story must feel real and specific (use details: names, dates, places, numbers)
- The divergence must be caused by a decision, not luck — the reader needs to believe they can make the same choice
- The pivot must feel natural, not forced — the product enters as a natural part of the story
- Never start selling before identification is complete — if you pivot too early, the story feels like a trick

### Best For
- Founder letters ("I was in your shoes...")
- About pages
- Long-form sales pages
- Case study narratives
- Email welcome sequences

## 4. The Yes Ladder

Build to the big ask through a series of small agreements. Each "yes" makes the next one easier. Requires deep customer empathy — you need to know exactly what they already believe.

From the Notion Mastery sales page and direct response tradition.

### Structure

1. **Start with an undeniable truth** — Something the audience already believes. "You've tried to get organized before."
2. **Build agreement** — Add another truth they recognize. "And it worked for about a week."
3. **Name their frustration** — Show you understand their specific pain. "Then the system fell apart because it was too rigid for how you actually work."
4. **Introduce the insight** — A new way of thinking about the problem. "The issue isn't discipline. It's that most systems fight against your natural workflow."
5. **Present your solution** — Now that they're nodding, show how your product embodies the insight. "Notion Mastery works because it builds around YOU."
6. **The ask** — By now, saying yes feels like the natural next step.

### Rules

- Each statement must be genuinely true — one false note breaks the entire ladder
- The audience must already agree with each rung before you say it
- The insight (step 4) is the key moment — it must feel like an "aha," not a sales pitch
- The ask must feel like a logical conclusion, not a pivot

### Best For
- Sales pages for complex/expensive products
- Email sequences (each email = one rung)
- Webinar scripts
- Nurture sequences where trust is low

## 5. Hero's Journey / Advertorial

Copy structured like a TV episode: introduce the hero, show the adversity, deliver the transformation. The reader identifies with the hero and wants the same outcome.

From the Hint Water advertorial and Day 10's analysis of editorial-style ads.

### Structure

1. **Meet the Hero** — Introduce a real person (founder, customer, or reader stand-in) in a relatable situation
2. **The Problem** — Show the adversity they faced. Make it vivid and specific.
3. **The Struggle** — Failed attempts, frustration, mounting stakes. This builds empathy.
4. **The Discovery** — How they found the solution (your product). Must feel organic, not planted.
5. **The Transformation** — Show the concrete results. Before/after with specifics.
6. **The Invitation** — Invite the reader to start their own journey

### Advertorial-Specific Rules

- Write it like an article, not an ad — the reader should be 3 paragraphs in before they realize it's selling something
- Use editorial voice (third person or interview style)
- Include real details that a marketer wouldn't think to include (these signal authenticity)
- The product should feel like a natural part of the story, not the point of the story
- End with a soft CTA that continues the editorial tone

### Best For
- Advertorials (native ads, sponsored content)
- Founder story pages
- Case studies
- About pages
- Email sequences (the "journey" unfolds over multiple emails)

## Framework Selection Matrix

Use this to choose the right framework based on your situation:

| Situation | Best Framework | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| **Cold audience, unknown product** | Hero's Journey / Advertorial | Story builds trust before selling |
| **Cold audience, known category** | AIDA | Classic structure that earns attention first |
| **Warm audience, complex product** | Yes Ladder | Build agreement before the big ask |
| **Warm audience, simple product** | AIDA (compressed) | They already know; just desire + action |
| **Long-form sales page** | Slippery Slope + AIDA | Slope keeps them reading; AIDA structures the sell |
| **Email sequence** | Story-Sell or Yes Ladder | Unfolds over multiple touchpoints |
| **Founder letter / about page** | Story-Sell or Hero's Journey | Personal narrative creates identification |
| **Ad copy (short-form)** | AIDA (compressed) | Limited space; every word must earn its place |
| **Advertorial / native ad** | Hero's Journey | Reads like editorial content |
| **Product description** | AIDA or PAS | Quick problem-solution structure |
| **Sales-oriented landing page** | Slippery Slope + Yes Ladder | Narrative flow + building agreement |

### Combining Frameworks

Frameworks aren't mutually exclusive. The best copy often combines:
- **Slippery Slope** as the connective tissue (transitions, open loops, curiosity gaps) with **AIDA** as the macro structure
- **Story-Sell** for the intro/hook with **Yes Ladder** for the body
- **Hero's Journey** for the narrative with **AIDA** for the CTA section

The framework is a scaffold, not a prison. Use it to structure your thinking, then write naturally.
