# Swipe File Method

The copywork method — learning to write great copy by studying great copy. This is the unique mechanic that differentiates this agent: before writing, it researches and analyzes the best examples of the target copy type.

Source: Sam Parr's Copy That curriculum — Day 0 (Copywork introduction), reinforced throughout all 10 days

---

## Why Copywork Works

Copywork is how every great copywriter learned the craft. You study the masters by:
1. **Reading** great copy with analytical eyes
2. **Breaking down** WHY it works (not just that it works)
3. **Copying** it by hand to internalize rhythm, pacing, and structure
4. **Adapting** the patterns to your own product and voice

This isn't plagiarism — it's pattern recognition. A jazz musician learns standards before improvising. A painter copies masters before developing their own style. A copywriter studies the greats before writing original work.

### What It Trains

- **Rhythm**: How great copywriters vary sentence length and pacing
- **Structure**: How they organize ideas for maximum impact
- **Hooks**: How they open to grab attention
- **Transitions**: How they keep readers moving forward
- **Voice**: How they sound human and confident
- **Persuasion**: How they create desire without being pushy

---

## How the Agent Uses Swipe (Phase 2)

During the Write workflow, Phase 2 (Research & Swipe) works like this:

1. **Identify the copy type** from Phase 1 (sales page, email sequence, etc.)
2. **Search for best-in-class examples** using WebSearch/WebFetch:
   - Search for award-winning examples of this copy type
   - Look for examples from companies in the same industry/category
   - Find examples recommended by copywriting experts
3. **Analyze 2-3 examples** using the Swipe Analysis Template below
4. **Extract patterns** that apply to the current project
5. **Save analysis** to `copy/swipe-notes.md` for reference during drafting

The goal is NOT to copy these examples. The goal is to understand what makes them work, then apply those principles to the user's specific product, audience, and voice.

## Swipe Analysis Template

Use this template when breaking down each example:

```markdown
## [Example Name] — [Company/Author]

**Source**: [URL or description]
**Copy Type**: [sales page, email, etc.]
**Why It's Great**: [1-2 sentence summary of what makes it exceptional]

### Framework Used
[AIDA / Slippery Slope / Story-Sell / Yes Ladder / Hero's Journey / Hybrid]

### Hook Analysis
- **Hook type**: [Bold claim / Objection handler / Story / Question / Stat / etc.]
- **Opening line**: "[exact text]"
- **Why it works**: [analysis]

### Slippery Slope Score
- **Paragraph transitions**: [1-5 score, with notes on strongest transitions]
- **Open loops**: [identified open loops and where they close]
- **Exit ramps**: [places where a reader could stop — and why they don't]

### Desire Creation
- **How desire is built**: [technique analysis]
- **Benefit-of-benefit used?**: [yes/no + example]
- **Emotional drivers**: [fear, aspiration, frustration, curiosity, etc.]

### CTA
- **CTA text**: "[exact text]"
- **Risk reversal**: [guarantee, free trial, etc.]
- **Urgency**: [real or manufactured?]

### Readability
- **Approximate grade level**: [4-8 target]
- **Sentence variation**: [good/needs work]
- **Voice**: [conversational/formal/editorial]

### Patterns to Steal
1. [Specific technique to adapt]
2. [Specific technique to adapt]
3. [Specific technique to adapt]
```

## Canonical Examples from the Course

These are the reference pieces studied throughout the Copy That curriculum. When analyzing new swipe examples, compare against these benchmarks.

### 1. The Wall Street Journal "Two Young Men" Letter
- **Type**: Sales letter (subscription)
- **Framework**: Story-Sell
- **What to study**: The parallel story structure, the pivot from story to product, the naturalism of the sell
- **Revenue**: $2 billion+ over its lifetime
- **Day**: 5

### 2. Gary Halbert's AIDA Letters
- **Type**: Direct mail / sales letters
- **Framework**: AIDA
- **What to study**: How Halbert opens (always with a hook that earns the first sentence), how he builds desire through specifics, how he closes with irresistible offers
- **Day**: 2

### 3. Ben Franklin's Autobiography Opening
- **Type**: Narrative / memoir
- **Framework**: Slippery Slope
- **What to study**: How each sentence compels you to read the next without any tricks — just pure momentum through interesting, specific detail
- **Day**: 3

### 4. Joel Spolsky's Blog Posts
- **Type**: Technical blog / essay
- **Framework**: Slippery Slope
- **What to study**: How an engineering blog can be unputdownable through curiosity gaps, storytelling, and relentless forward momentum
- **Day**: 3

### 5. Notion Mastery Sales Page
- **Type**: Course sales page
- **Framework**: Yes Ladder
- **What to study**: How it builds agreement through small yeses before asking for the purchase. Each section is a truth the reader already believes.
- **Day**: 9

### 6. Hint Water Advertorial
- **Type**: Advertorial / native ad
- **Framework**: Hero's Journey
- **What to study**: How it reads like a magazine article for 80% of its length, how the product enters the story naturally, how the CTA feels like a continuation rather than a pivot
- **Day**: 10

### 7. Sam Parr's "The Hustle" Emails
- **Type**: Newsletter / email
- **Framework**: Conversational Voice + Slippery Slope
- **What to study**: Conversational tone, rhythm, personality, and how to make informational content feel like a message from a friend
- **Day**: 6

### 8. Stephen King's Writing Voice
- **Type**: Prose / narrative
- **Framework**: Writing mechanics
- **What to study**: Simple words, active voice, no adverbs, strong verbs. The Hemingway approach to fiction.
- **Day**: 4

### 9. Scott Adams' Blog Posts
- **Type**: Persuasion essays
- **Framework**: Clarity rules
- **What to study**: How to make complex ideas simple and persuasive through short sentences, common words, and logical progression
- **Day**: 4

### 10. The "Forgotten Copy" Examples (Day 8)
- **Type**: Unsubscribe pages, error pages, welcome emails, about pages
- **Framework**: Brand personality + surprise
- **What to study**: How small moments of copy (404 pages, confirmation emails, unsubscribe text) can reinforce brand and delight users
- **Day**: 8

## Building a Swipe File

For users who want to build their own swipe file over time:

### What to Save
- Copy that made you stop scrolling
- Emails you actually read all the way through
- Sales pages where you almost bought something you didn't need
- Ad copy that made you click
- Error pages or forgotten copy that made you smile

### How to Save
- Screenshot or save the full text
- Note the date you found it
- Tag by copy type (sales page, email, ad, etc.)
- Write one sentence: "I saved this because ______"

### Where to Save
- `copy/swipe/{copy-type}/` directory in the project
- Each file: `{company}-{brief-description}.md`

### When to Reference
- Before writing any new piece of copy (Phase 2)
- When stuck on a hook or opening
- When the copy feels flat and needs inspiration
- During review mode, to benchmark against great examples
