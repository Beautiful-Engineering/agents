# Writing Mechanics

The craft of making copy readable, rhythmic, conversational, and the right length. These mechanics apply to every copy type — they're the difference between copy that technically says the right things and copy that FEELS right.

Source: Sam Parr's Copy That curriculum — Days 1 (Rhythm), 4 (Hemingway Score), 6 (Conversational Voice), 7 (Copy Length)

---

## Rhythm & Flow

Great copy has a beat. Short sentences punch. Long sentences flow and carry the reader along like a river, building momentum until the next short sentence hits. Like that.

### Sentence Variation

- **Short sentences** (3-8 words): Create impact. Use for key points, transitions, emotional beats.
- **Medium sentences** (9-18 words): Carry information. The workhorses of your copy.
- **Long sentences** (19-30 words): Build momentum, create flow, connect complex ideas — but only when the reader is already engaged.

**The rule**: Never stack more than two sentences of the same length. Vary constantly.

### Pacing Techniques

- **Staccato burst**: Three short sentences in a row for emphasis. Use sparingly. Like this. Got it?
- **The long-short combo**: A flowing sentence that builds and builds and carries the reader along... then stops. Impact.
- **The single-word paragraph**: Devastating. (Use once per piece, maximum.)
- **The list break**: When you've been writing flowing paragraphs, drop a list. It gives the eye a rest and the brain a reset.

### Rhythm Diagnostic

Read your copy aloud. If you:
- Get bored → sentences are too uniform in length
- Run out of breath → sentences are too long
- Feel choppy → too many short sentences stacked
- Can't find the key point → it's buried in a long sentence

## Hemingway Score

Target grade 4-8 readability. If Ernest Hemingway, Stephen King, and Scott Adams agree on one thing, it's this: simple writing is hard, and it's the only kind worth reading.

### The Rules

| Rule | Why | How |
|------|-----|-----|
| **No passive voice** | Passive voice hides the actor and kills energy | "The button was clicked" → "She clicked the button" |
| **Cut adverbs** | Adverbs are a sign the verb is too weak | "ran quickly" → "sprinted" / "very good" → "excellent" |
| **Simple words** | Big words don't make you sound smart | "utilize" → "use" / "implement" → "do" / "leverage" → "use" |
| **Short paragraphs** | Walls of text kill readability | Max 3-4 sentences per paragraph. Often 1-2. |
| **Active verbs** | Strong verbs carry sentences | "is responsible for managing" → "manages" |

### Stephen King's Toolbox (from On Writing)

- **Vocabulary**: Use the first word that comes to mind. If it's good enough, it's good enough.
- **Grammar**: Know the rules, then break them intentionally. Never break them out of ignorance.
- **The adverb**: "The road to hell is paved with adverbs." Cut them all, add back only the ones you'd die on a hill for.
- **Passive voice**: "Timid writers like passive voice for the same reason timid lovers like passive partners." Be active.

### Scott Adams' Clarity Rules

- Write short sentences
- Use simple words the reader already knows
- If it requires an explanation, cut it or rewrite it
- Avoid jargon — even if your audience knows the jargon, simple words read faster
- One idea per sentence

### Readability Quick Check

Before declaring any draft done:
1. Paste into a readability scorer (Hemingway Editor, readable.com)
2. Target: Grade 4-8
3. Passive voice: < 5% of sentences
4. Average sentence length: 12-18 words
5. Adverb count: < 1 per 100 words

## Write Like You Speak

Copy that sounds written sounds fake. Copy that sounds spoken sounds human. The goal is conversational — like you're explaining this to a smart friend over coffee.

### Conversational Markers

Use these to make copy sound human:

- **Contractions**: "you're" not "you are" / "don't" not "do not" / "it's" not "it is"
- **Fragments**: "Not bad." / "Pretty simple, actually." / "The catch?"
- **Rhetorical questions**: "Sound familiar?" / "Know what happens next?"
- **Direct address**: "you" and "your" — talk TO the reader, not AT them
- **Informal transitions**: "Here's the thing." / "So." / "But wait." / "Look."
- **Parenthetical asides**: (the kind you'd whisper to a friend)
- **First person**: "I" and "we" — be a person, not a brand

### The Read-Aloud Test

This is the single most important quality check for any copy:

1. Read your copy out loud — actually out loud, not in your head
2. Mark every place where you:
   - Stumble over words
   - Would say it differently in conversation
   - Sound like a brochure, press release, or AI
   - Use a word you'd never say to a friend
3. Rewrite those sections as you'd actually say them
4. Read aloud again

**If it sounds weird to say, it sounds weird to read.**

### Voice Traps to Avoid

| Trap | Example | Fix |
|------|---------|-----|
| **Corporate speak** | "We leverage cutting-edge solutions" | "We built a tool that actually works" |
| **AI voice** | "In today's fast-paced digital landscape" | Cut the entire sentence |
| **Academic** | "It is important to note that..." | Just say the thing |
| **Hedge words** | "We believe that perhaps..." | Take a stance |
| **Empty superlatives** | "The best, most innovative, world-class" | Replace with a specific claim |
| **Abstraction** | "Optimize your workflow efficiency" | "Get your work done in half the time" |

## Copy Length Principle

The right length is: **as long as it takes to achieve the action.** Not shorter. Not longer.

### Length Factors

| Factor | Shorter Copy | Longer Copy |
|--------|-------------|-------------|
| **Product familiarity** | Known category (another CRM) | New category (first of its kind) |
| **Price point** | Low / free | High / enterprise |
| **Commitment level** | Email signup | Annual contract |
| **Audience awareness** | Problem-aware, solution-aware | Unaware, problem-unaware |
| **Trust level** | Warm referral, known brand | Cold traffic, unknown brand |
| **Complexity** | Simple product, obvious value | Complex product, needs explanation |
| **Risk** | Low risk, easy to reverse | High risk, hard to undo |

### Guidelines by Copy Type

| Copy Type | Typical Length | Why |
|-----------|---------------|-----|
| Ad copy (social) | 1-3 sentences | Limited attention, goal is click |
| Product description | 50-200 words | Quick scan, decision support |
| Email (single) | 200-500 words | One idea, one action |
| Welcome email | 100-300 words | They already signed up; don't oversell |
| Sales page (low-ticket) | 500-1500 words | Enough to desire + overcome objections |
| Sales page (high-ticket) | 2000-5000+ words | Must build trust, handle many objections |
| Founder letter | 500-1500 words | Story-driven, must feel authentic not padded |
| Advertorial | 1000-3000 words | Reads like an article, needs narrative space |
| About page | 300-800 words | Focused story, not a biography |

### The "Is It Done?" Test

Your copy is the right length when:
1. Every sentence earns its place (cut any sentence — does the copy suffer?)
2. Every objection is handled (could the reader say "but what about..."?)
3. The reader has enough desire to take the action
4. The reader has enough confidence to take the action
5. Nothing is repeated without purpose

If you can cut a paragraph and nothing is lost, cut it. If the reader would still have questions, you're too short.

## Readability Checklist

Run this on every draft before presenting to the user:

### Structure
- [ ] Paragraphs are 1-4 sentences max
- [ ] Subheadings break content every 200-300 words
- [ ] Lists are used for 3+ parallel items
- [ ] Bold is used for key phrases (not whole sentences)
- [ ] White space between sections

### Language
- [ ] Grade 4-8 readability
- [ ] Passive voice < 5%
- [ ] Average sentence length 12-18 words
- [ ] No jargon without explanation
- [ ] Contractions used naturally
- [ ] Active verbs throughout

### Flow
- [ ] Sentence lengths vary (short/medium/long)
- [ ] Transitions connect paragraphs (no orphaned ideas)
- [ ] No two consecutive paragraphs start the same way
- [ ] Read-aloud test passed (no stumbles, no brochure voice)

### Voice
- [ ] Sounds like a person, not a company
- [ ] "You" appears more than "we"
- [ ] No AI cliches ("in today's...", "it's important to note...", "leverage")
- [ ] Takes a clear stance (no hedge words)
- [ ] Matches brand personality (if Story System exists)
