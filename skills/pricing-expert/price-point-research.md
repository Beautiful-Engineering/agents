# Price Point Research

## Three Pricing Approaches

Most companies use a mix. Each has tradeoffs.

| Method | What It Is | Pros | Cons |
|--------|-----------|------|------|
| **Value-based** | Price based on the value customers believe they get | Most customer-driven; best aligned to JTBD; helps avoid underpricing | Requires customer input/data; not exact science |
| **Cost-plus** | Price = cost + target profit margin | Simple math; guarantees margin | Ignores WTP; risks undervaluing product |
| **Competitor-based** | Price in line with (or against) competitors | Quick; keeps you aligned with market | Assumes competitors got it right; ignores your differentiation |

## Van Westendorp 4-Question Method

Ask prospects or customers these four questions to triangulate a reasonable price band:

1. At what price would this feel **too expensive** to ever consider?
2. At what price would this start to feel **expensive, but still worth considering**?
3. At what price would this feel like a **good deal**?
4. At what price would this feel **so cheap** you'd question the quality?

### How to Use Results

- **With 100+ survey responses**: Plot the four curves on a graph. The intersection of "too expensive" and "good deal" gives the optimal price point. The range between "cheap" and "expensive but worth it" is the acceptable price band.
- **With <100 responses (interviews)**: Use directional input. Look for clustering patterns in answers. No graphs needed — ballpark is sufficient.

### Survey Best Practices

- Offer price **ranges or sliders** to avoid wildly off numbers
- Test the survey with colleagues before shipping
- Use a platform with built-in Van Westendorp tools (e.g. SurveyKing)

## Max-Diff Method (for Value Metric Validation)

Use when you have 100+ survey responses and need to determine which features/metrics matter most.

Instead of numerical ratings (1-10), present sets of features and ask which they value **most** and **least**. Reveals relative priorities and power features.

### When to Use
- Choosing between multiple value metric proxies
- Validating which features belong in which tier
- Identifying upgrade triggers

## Three Universal Price Anchors

### A) Value Equation Anchor

Co-build a value equation with the customer, then price as a fair share.

> "If [your solution] delivers [specific outcome] worth [dollar value], charging [X% of that value] is reasonable."

**Examples**:
- **Sales tools**: 3 meetings/month x 10% close rate x $20k ACV = $6k value. $1k-$2k monthly is fair.
- **Marketing tools**: 2% conversion increase = $50k additional revenue/year. $500/month is 12% of value.
- **Operations tools**: 20 hours saved weekly x $100/hour = $8k/month savings. $800/month is 10%.

### B) Alternative Cost Anchor

Price against what they'd spend to solve the problem without you.

> "The alternative to [your solution] costs [higher amount]. Our price delivers [same/better outcome] with [additional benefits]."

**Examples**:
- **AI tools**: Full-time specialist costs $8k/month. AI delivers comparable results for $1k/month.
- **Software**: Building in-house costs $100k+ and 6 months. Your solution is $2k/month and works immediately.

### C) Competitor Comparison Anchor (Use with Caution)

Reference competitor pricing as a market reality check, not as your primary justification.

**When to use**: When prospects ask about competitors; to position within market range; to highlight differentiation.

**When NOT to use**: As primary justification; when competitors are clearly underpricing; when your value prop is significantly different.

## Hormozi Value Levers

Four levers from Alex Hormozi's "$100M Offers" that shift perceived value:

| Lever | Direction | Effect on Price |
|-------|-----------|----------------|
| **Dream Outcome** | Maximize | The more desirable the result, the more you can charge |
| **Perceived Likelihood** | Maximize | Higher confidence it will work = higher willingness to pay |
| **Time Delay** | Minimize | Faster results = more valuable |
| **Effort & Sacrifice** | Minimize | Less work required from customer = more valuable |

**Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)**

Each lever you strengthen gives more room to charge and hold firm on price.

## The 10x Value Test

Rule of thumb: Customers should feel they get roughly 10x the price in value.

- **Your price = ~10% of the value you create**
- **Their ROI = ~900% (10x return)**

**Examples**:
- Save them $10k/month → Price ~$1k/month
- Generate $50k additional revenue → Price ~$5k
- Replace $20k/month in labor costs → Price ~$2k/month

## Price Band Triangulation

Combine all three methods to find your price range:

```
1. Value-based floor: [10x test result]
2. Van Westendorp band: [cheap threshold] — [expensive-but-worth-it threshold]
3. Cost-plus floor: [costs + target margin]
4. Competitor range: [lowest competitor] — [highest competitor]
5. Alternative cost ceiling: [cost of best alternative]

→ Target price band: [intersection of all five]
```
