# Landing Page Anatomy

The 80/20 landing page structure, section-by-section copywriting guide, and alternative page types.

Source: `make-high-converting-landing-pages/02-anatomy.md` through `15-other-types.md`, `copywriting-fundamentals/03-frameworks.md`, `copywriting-fundamentals/05-hooks.md`, `create-your-story-system/07-value-props.md`, `create-your-story-system/08-hooks.md`

---

## The Core Formula

**Purchase Rate = (Desire + Confidence) / (Effort + Confusion)**

Every element on the page either increases the numerator or decreases the denominator:
- **Increase Desire**: Entice visitors with transformative value
- **Increase Confidence**: Make them confident they'll get that value
- **Decrease Effort**: Be concise, make every element count
- **Decrease Confusion**: Use clear, easily understood messaging

---

## Standard 80/20 Structure

This structure works for the vast majority of landing pages. Creativity in conversion-focused funnels is generally bad — a confused person bounces.

| # | Section | Primary Goal |
|---|---------|-------------|
| 1 | Navbar | Navigation + sticky CTA |
| 2 | Hero | Hook + describe what you sell |
| 3 | Social Proof #1 | Logos / stats (quick confidence) |
| 4 | Features / Benefits / Objections | 3-6 value props with objection handling |
| 5 | Social Proof #2 | Testimonials / case studies / results |
| 6 | FAQ | Catch-all for remaining objections |
| 7 | CTA Section | Dedicated conversion push |
| 8 | Footer | Legal + navigation |

Use this same structure for persona-specific, ad-specific, keyword-specific, funnel-specific, and problem-specific landing pages — just change the words and images.

---

## Section 1: Navbar

- Sticky navigation with logo and key links
- Include a CTA button in the navbar (always visible)
- For conversion-focused pages, strip out non-essential links — don't let visitors get lost

---

## Section 2: Hero

The most important section. Determines whether visitors keep reading or leave.

**Hero elements:**
- Header text (required)
- Subheader text (required)
- CTA button (required)
- Image or visual (strongly recommended)

People don't have short attention spans — they have short **consideration** spans. They must be hooked in seconds.

### Writing the Header

**Step 1: Make it fully descriptive**
- Litmus test: If visitors read ONLY the header, will they know exactly what you sell?
- Avoid slogans like "Improve your workflow!" — useless
- Good: "Visually design and develop sites from scratch. No coding."
- Good: "Groceries delivered in 1 hour."

**Step 2: Identify how users get value**
- What bad alternative do people use without your product?
- How is your product better?
- Turn it into an action statement (the value prop)

**Step 3: Add a hook — choose one:**

**Option A: Bold, specific claim**
- Highly specific, triggers "wow, I didn't know that was possible"
- Instead of "Speed up your marketing" → "Get 2x more leads without increasing your ad spend"

**Option B: Address the #1 objection**
- Identify major buying objections (from customer surveys)
- Proactively address in the header
- "Build your own website. No coding required." (addresses "But I don't know how to code")

### Writing the Subheader

- Complements the header — don't repeat it
- Explains HOW the product works OR which features make the bold claim believable
- One to two sentences maximum
- Don't make it an essay

### Hero Image

- Show the product in action or visually represent its value
- Adding people is beneficial (research-backed)
- Don't use generic stock photos or random illustrations
- For software: screenshots or short demos
- For physical products: product in use

---

## Section 3: Social Proof #1 (Logos)

Quick-hit confidence builder. A collage of logos showing:
- Press coverage (As seen in...)
- Well-known customers
- For ecommerce: customer count or review aggregate ("50,000+ happy customers")

**Goals**: Make it seem like everyone already knows about you + increase confidence.

If you don't have noteworthy customers yet: provide the product free to people at well-known companies. If they use it, place their logos.

Can be built into the hero section for above-the-fold impact.

---

## Section 4: Features / Benefits / Objections

3-6 feature blocks. Each includes:
- **Header**: A short value prop (not a feature name)
- **Paragraph**: Explain the value prop + handle one objection
- **Image**: Reinforce the value prop visually
- **CTA button**: Tailored to the specific feature when possible

### Benefits vs. Features

Most companies mess up by listing features. Focus on benefits.
- Features are about the product
- Benefits are about the customer
- The customer is the protagonist; the product is the supporting character

**Example**: Apple focuses on the benefit (safety from SOS/Crash detection) then explains the features that achieve it.

### Feature Header Formula

- Write a short value prop, not a feature label
- Avoid vague language like "Empower your sales"
- Bluntly describe what the customer gets
- For a portable grill: "Cooks and sears" / "No clean up" / "Cooks more than just meat"

### Feature Paragraph

- Concisely describe the feature
- Address one important objection preventing purchase
- If complex, link to a details page or use tooltips — keep users in the flow

### Feature Image

- Must be visually appealing (modern, clear)
- Must demonstrate product value (show in action, not abstract)
- For software: GIFs of product in action, blur tiny text
- For physical goods: use cases and close-ups of build quality

### Carry the Narrative

Each benefit should tie back to the dominant value prop in the hero. Create callbacks to reinforce the main message throughout.

---

## Section 5: Social Proof #2 (Testimonials)

Uses testimonials, reviews, and case studies to achieve all four goals:
1. Make it seem everyone knows about you
2. Increase confidence they can deliver
3. Highlight transformative value others received
4. Make clear who it's for and what it does

### Ideal Reviews

- Highlight the specific value they got
- Call out and handle common objections
- Come from trustworthy people or companies
- Include photo of reviewer or company logo

### Formats

- **Collage of reviews**: Great for high volume
- **Hand-picked customer results**: Great for selective sharing / lower volume

---

## Section 6: FAQ

People skim, even for high-stakes decisions. FAQ is a catch-all in an easy-to-scan format.

**What to cover:**
- All benefits and features (yes, repeat them)
- Common questions from support team
- Common objections
- Who it's for and who it's NOT for
- Refund policies
- Pricing details

---

## Section 7: CTA Section

The CTA is the actionable next step to fulfill the claim in your header.

**Strong CTAs:**
- Continue the narrative from the hero: "Find food" / "Start learning"
- Reiterate the strongest value prop
- Include a reason to act now

**Weak CTAs:**
- "Request a meeting" (vague — what product is this?)
- "Submit" / "Sign up" (no value communicated)

**Match CTA to traffic temperature:**
- Cold traffic → newsletter / free trial / lead magnet
- Warm / retargeting traffic → purchase / demo

---

## Section 8: Footer

- Copyright + privacy policy + terms (legally required)
- Links to key pages (pricing, features, about, contact)
- For conversion-focused pages: strip non-essential links

---

## 7 Questions Every Landing Page Must Answer

1. **What is the product?** Explain like you're talking to grandma. No fluffy words.
2. **Is it right for me?** Make clear whose problem it solves.
3. **Is it legit?** Invest in design. Social proof helps.
4. **Who else is using it?** People buy when respected people/companies are customers.
5. **How much?** Tell them the cost. Don't waste time with sticker shock after a sales demo.
6. **Where can I get help?** Make support obvious. Shows legitimacy.
7. **How do I take action?** Make the next step obvious. Match CTA to traffic temperature.

## 7 Copywriting Frameworks for LP Sections

Use these for feature blocks, CTA sections, and persuasive passages:

### 1. PAS(P) — Problem, Agitation, Solution, Proof
Great for feature blocks and CTA sections.

### 2. BAB — Before, After, Bridge
Good for hero subheaders and transformation-focused sections.

### 3. AIDA — Attention, Interest, Desire, Action
Good for CTA sections.

### 4. Storytelling (Pixar Framework)
Powerful for case study sections and pre-sale pages.

### 5. PASTOR — Problem, Amplify, Solution, Testimony, Offer, Response
Extended PAS. Good for long-form sales pages.

### 6. The 4 P's — Promise, Picture, Proof, Push
Good for benefit-focused feature blocks.

### 7. PRUNE — Point, Reason, Unveil, Nail, Exit
Uses analogy to drive the point home.

## 10 Hook Types for Hero Headers

### 1. Credibility
Tell people WHY they should listen to you. Experience, effort, or someone else's credibility.

### 2. Fear (FOMO/FOBO)
Loss aversion and negativity bias. "If you're not doing X..."

### 3. Curiosity
Create an information gap they must fill. Open loops.

### 4. Counter-Narrative
Contradict what people believe. Spiky points of view.

### 5. Eloquence
Voice an unvoiced concern. "Finally, someone said it!"

### 6. Faces (visual)
Facial expressions in hero images. Hardwired to notice.

### 7. Value
Simply promise useful information. "Here are the 7 strategies..."

### 8. Surprise
Lead with a surprising fact or statistic.

### 9. Celebration
Celebrate a milestone or achievement.

### 10. Identity
Signal who the page is for. "Attention startup founders who..."

**Best practice**: Combine 2-3 hook types for the hero header.

## Value Prop Development (for LP Copy)

### Step 1: Three-Column Chart
| Bad Alternative | Value Prop | Target Persona |
|----------------|-----------|----------------|
| What people do without you + what makes it bad | How you're better (high-level) | Who cares most + top 2 reasons why |

### Step 2: Deep Value Prop Refinement
For each value prop, flesh out:
- **Problem**: Super-specific moment the audience experienced it (past tense)
- **Implications**: Bad things that happen after the problem (twist the knife)
- **Solution**: How your product solves it
- **Benefits**: Flip the implications — great things from the solution

### Step 3: Value Props → Headlines
Turn each refined value prop into a hero header candidate. Apply hook types to amplify.

## Harry Dry's 3 Rules of Good Copy

Apply to EVERY section:

1. **Can you visualize it?** Concrete beats abstract. "1,000 songs in your pocket" not "32GB storage."
2. **Can you falsify it?** Can the claim be proven true or false? "Quality you can trust" is meaningless.
3. **Can nobody else say this?** Is it unique to you? Generic claims don't differentiate.

**Three no's = rewrite. Three yes's = ship it.**

## Alternative Landing Page Types

Beyond the standard structure, consider these for specific campaigns:

1. **Interactive Calculator** — ROI/savings estimator → personalized result → CTA
2. **Competitor Comparison** — Direct head-to-head. Be honest — let competitor win where they actually do.
3. **Pre-Sales Page** — Story-driven warmup before product page. Good for cold traffic.
4. **Free Trial / Welcome Offer** — Exclusive welcome discount landing page.
5. **Quiz Funnel** — Reduce perceived risk through personalization. Great for products with customization.
6. **Advertorial** — Blog post that educates and introduces product naturally.
7. **Listicle** — "13 best X tools" with yours ranked first. Good for retargeting.

**Start small**: Pick one funnel type, create custom ads + landing pages, iterate from wins.
