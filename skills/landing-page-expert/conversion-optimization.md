## The Conversion Formula

**Purchase Rate = (Desire + Confidence) / (Effort + Confusion)**

Every change you make should move one of these four levers:

| Lever | Direction | How |
|-------|-----------|-----|
| Desire | Increase | Entice visitors with transformative value |
| Confidence | Increase | Social proof, specificity, credibility, testimonials |
| Effort | Decrease | Be concise, reduce steps, make CTAs obvious |
| Confusion | Decrease | Clear messaging, one message per page, logical flow |

## Rapid-Fire Conversion Tactics

### Device Optimization
Legible and functional across all devices (monitors, laptops, tablets, smartphones). Test on actual devices — responsive previews lie.

### Form as CTA
If the next step is subscribing or signing up, make the form itself the CTA — don't hide it behind a button that opens a modal. Reduce friction.

### Never Assume Anything
- Don't assume visitors understand your terms or acronyms
- Don't assume they know you or care about you
- Don't assume prior knowledge of the problem space
- Explain everything as if this is their first encounter

### Always Have CTAs Handy
Put CTAs in basically every section. Use a sticky navbar with a CTA button. Don't make visitors scroll to find the next step.

### Use Images
People understand images far faster than words. Show, don't tell. Product screenshots, demos, before/after visuals.

### Make It Skimmable
People don't read — they skim. Enable quick scanning to understand:
- Who it's for
- What you do
- Why it matters
Bold key phrases. Use bullet lists. Short paragraphs. Clear headers.

### Excessive Social Proof
More is always better. Logos, testimonials, case studies, review counts, press mentions — layer them throughout.

### Don't Assume Perfection
A/B test if you have traffic. Get feedback (Wynter, UserTesting) if you don't. No landing page is perfect on the first try.

## Copywriting Quality Check (Apply to Every Section)

### 11-Point Editing Checklist

1. **Make it about them** — Focus on what's in it for the reader
2. **Make it relatable** — Use analogies the reader already understands
3. **Cut the fluff** — Remove adverbs, adjectives, filler words
4. **Use simple words** — No jargon. Explain to a 10-year-old
5. **Be specific & visual** — Spell it out. Don't make them imagine
6. **Use active voice** — Subject does the action
7. **Tell a story** — Illustrate with examples. Minimum Viable Backstory
8. **Make it punchy** — Steal from poetry. Chop sentences. Add rhythm
9. **Handle objections** — "What almost stopped you from buying?"
10. **Be bold** — Take strong stances. No wishy-washy statements
11. **Appeal to interest, not reason** — Emotions drive action; logic rationalizes after

## A/B Testing Strategy

### Macro vs. Micro Testing

**Micro variants**: Minor adjustments (button color, CTA text, small copy tweaks)
- Small, quick changes
- Unlikely to have huge impact individually
- Combining several micros = a macro variant

**Macro variants**: Significant rethinking of the page
- Changing style completely, content ordering, targeting new audience
- **Always prioritize macros over micros — biggest ROI**
- Macro variants deliver big wins
- Micro variants tend to deliver only small wins

### The Cheap Macro Test

Test a new header + subheader + hero image. This one macro test can drastically change conversion since many people leave after skimming the top. The right combo of copy and image gets them to stick around.

### Big Swings Principle

Test things wildly different from each other, then double down on what works.

**Big swings**:
- Making the landing page 80% shorter
- Completely different hero messaging angle
- Video-first vs. text-first layout

**NOT big swings**:
- Changing button color
- Swapping one word in the CTA

Win big AND lose big. Keep winners, prune losers. Improve much faster over time.

### Structured A/B Testing Process

**Phase 1: Gather data** before brainstorming (don't test based on opinions):
- Self-assessment across 5 categories: Relevancy, Clarity, Value, Friction, Distraction
- Browse landing page inspiration (goodwebdesign.co.uk)
- Analyze competitor pages
- Review on-site behavior (Hotjar recordings)
- Sales team input (objections, common questions)
- User surveys
- Best-performing ad copy/creatives

**Phase 2: Generate ideas** — Proven macro strategies:
1. Mimic competitors' working sections (layout tactics, not words)
2. Write to a new persona (tailor messaging to different audience)
3. Cut the page in half (less content = more reading sometimes)
4. Take a stance (embrace one value prop more than competitors)
5. Combine micro-ideas into one macro change

**Phase 3: Prioritize and test**
- Build proposal: title, explanation, hypothesis, mockups
- Run 2-4 variants simultaneously
- A/B testing calendar: recurring event every 2 months to brainstorm macro variants

### Measuring Results

**Goal metric** (success/failure):
- Cost Per Acquisition (CPA)
- Return on Ad Spend (ROAS)

**Storytelling metrics** (clues to why):
- CTR → is the creative grabbing attention?
- CVR → does the landing page convert after click?
- CPC → is targeting too broad?

**Important**: Measure conversion boost primarily on **paid traffic** — that's where the delta is large enough to notice. Organic high-intent traffic has diminishing returns.

### Red Flags
- CTR stays below 0.3% after 1,000+ impressions
- CPA consistently 3x+ higher than target after real spend
- CTR drops steadily over time (creative fatigue)

## Landing Page Feedback Framework

Get feedback from two types of reviewers:

**People NOT in your market:**
- Test how appealing and comprehensible the copy is
- Reveals if you over-assume base-level knowledge

**People IN your market:**
- Test if messaging is unique and descriptive enough to beat competitors

### 7 Assessment Criteria

Score each 1-10:

1. **Conversion**: Would you hand over your credit card right now? If not, what would change that?
2. **Interest**: Rate sustained interest 1-10. What should be rewritten to improve it?
3. **Clarity**: What's unclear? What unanswered questions are left?
4. **Expansion**: Did you find something you wanted more details on?
5. **Brevity**: If you had to delete half the page, which half?
6. **Disbelief**: What triggered your "That's scammy sales speak!" reflex?
7. **Objections**: What would stop you from taking action?

## Benefit of the Benefit

When writing about product value, find the second-order benefit:

1. List all product benefits AND downsides
2. For each: "What's an unexpected/obvious/funny/convenient benefit of this benefit/downside?"
3. The second-order benefit is often the real selling point

**Examples**:
- Trampoline → fun for kids → **parents get peace and quiet**
- Electric car → no gas stations → **don't care when gas prices spike**
- Sports car → no back seats (downside) → **no room for kids** (benefit for some)

Push to the second or third order. That's where real resonance lives.
