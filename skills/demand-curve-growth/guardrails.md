# Growth Guardrails

Guardrails define the boundaries of your growth strategy: where you're going, what constrains you, and what's financially viable. They are the three inputs that "shrink the problem" before you design your growth engine.

---

## Part 1: Three Goal Types

### Learning Goals
The riskiest assumptions and untested hypotheses that must be validated.

**Fundamental questions every startup must answer:**
1. Have we built a product our market actually wants? (PMF)
2. Can we consistently and predictably attract more users? (Acquisition)
3. Do we have a viable business model? (Monetization)

**Stage-dependent learning goals (post-PMF signals):**
- **Scale**: "Can we scale acquisition 3X without breaking model-channel alignment?"
- **Efficiency**: "Can we reduce CAC below $Y to make the business sustainable?"
- **Model fit**: "Can we validate our monetization strategy and unit economics?"
- **Brand fit**: "Can we find messaging so users instantly 'get it' before using the product?"

**Question to ask**: In a sentence or two, what is the key assumption or hypothesis you need to validate at this stage?

### Financial Goals
Financial performance milestones — both short-term and long-term.

**Short-term goal archetypes:**
- **Bootstrapped**: Reach cash flow sustainability ASAP
- **Pre-seed/Seed**: Build a traction story for fundraising
- **VC-backed**: Hit aggressive growth targets (revenue or user base)

**Goal categories:**
- Efficiency goals (achieving profitability)
- Growth rate goals (20% MoM user growth)
- Scale goals ($1M → $5M ARR)

**Questions to ask:**
1. What is your specific short-term financial/performance goal?
2. What is your long-term goal? (e.g., profitable $5M ARR business vs. next unicorn)

### Personal Goals
How you want to go about reaching your goals — the tradeoffs you are and are NOT willing to make.

**Why this matters**: Many founders default to "growth at all costs" without examining alignment. Define your rules of the game before building a system misaligned with your values.

**Question to ask**: What are you willing to sacrifice to achieve your long-term goal? Does the upside outweigh the tradeoffs (fundraising pressure, large teams, investor management)?

## Part 2: Constraints (5 Categories)

Constraints are filters that clarify what's available when designing your growth engine.

### Resourcing Constraints
- Monthly budget available for growth
- Hours per week available for growth work
- Team capacity and in-house skills (also: team-strategy fit from enjoyment/interest perspective)

### Regulatory & Legal Constraints
- Industry compliance (HIPAA, financial disclosure)
- Ad placement limitations (cannabis, crypto, alcohol)
- Legal risks (lawsuits from incumbents)

### Brand Constraints
- Reputation sensitivity (avoid spammy placements, aggressive tactics)
- Premium positioning (can't use discounts, aggressive remarketing)

### Timing Constraints
- Urgent timeline (limited runway, fundraising deadline)
- Seasonal windows (back-to-school, holiday)

### Audience Constraints
- Niche TAM (very small total addressable market)
- Hard-to-reach audience (rural contractors, Gen Z physicians)
- Trust-sensitive segment (health, finance, parenting)

**Question to ask**: For each category, what constraints apply to your business right now?

## Part 3: Growth Economics (6-Step Calculation)

The financial "physics" of your growth engine — determines what is and isn't viable.

### Step 1: Calculate ARPU
ARPU = Total annual revenue / Number of paying customers

- Use revenue, not profit (margin applied next)
- Exclude sales tax and refunds
- Early stage: estimate from current pricing and average behavior

### Step 2: Adjust for Gross Margin
Margin-adjusted ARPU = ARPU x Gross Margin %

Example: $240 ARPU x 80% = $192 margin-adjusted ARPU

### Step 3: Map Revenue Timing
Create a 12-month table showing when margin-adjusted revenue arrives for a typical customer. Account for:
- Free trials (30 days)
- Freemium to paid lag
- Usage-based ramp over first months

### Step 4: Choose Payback Period
How long it takes margin-adjusted revenue to recover CAC.

**Guidelines:**
- **3 months**: Best for bootstrapped consumer startups prioritizing efficiency
- **6 months**: Target for consumer tech startups (keeps cash flowing for high-velocity acquisition)
- **12+ months**: Generally not advised for consumer unless heavily funded and grabbing market share

### Step 5: Calculate Target CAC
**Formula**: Target CAC = ARPU x Gross Margin x (Payback Months / 12)

**Quick reference:**
- 3-month payback ≈ 4:1 ARPU:CAC
- 6-month payback ≈ 2:1 ARPU:CAC
- 12-month payback ≈ 1:1 ARPU:CAC

### Step 6: Set Growth Budget
Three frameworks:
- **Target-Based (top-down)**: Budget = Target Customers x Target CAC
- **Runway-Constrained (bottom-up)**: Allocate 10-20% of OPEX to growth
- **Revenue % (steady-state)**: 5-20% of annual revenue for early-stage

## Questions Sequence (Ordered)

1. What is your primary learning goal — what assumption must you validate?
2. What is your short-term financial goal (revenue, profit, customers)?
3. What is your long-term performance goal?
4. What are your personal goals — what tradeoffs are you NOT willing to make?
5. What are your resourcing constraints (budget, time, team)?
6. Any regulatory/legal constraints?
7. Any brand constraints?
8. Any timing constraints?
9. Any audience constraints?
10. What is your ARPU?
11. What is your gross margin?
12. What payback period are you targeting?
13. What is your Target CAC? (calculate together)
