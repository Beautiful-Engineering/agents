The most valuable skill of the next decade won't be coding.

It'll be knowing how to build things.

Those sound the same. They're not.

Coding is writing software by hand, line by line. Building is knowing what to create, why it matters, and how to get it into the world. Then using whatever tools exist to make it happen.

Right now, the tools that exist are insane.

I've been finding that out the hard way for years. No CS degree. No bootcamp. I started with no-code tools, hit their ceiling, and kept going. Taught myself how to work in terminals, how to think about architecture, how to build alongside engineers every day. Slowly, stubbornly, I became technical.

But I never forgot what it felt like to not be. The confusion of staring at a terminal with no idea where to start. The frustration of knowing exactly what you want to build but not having the skills to build it. I remember every wall I hit. I still hit them.

That's honestly why I think I can help. I've been on both sides. I know what technical people take for granted, and I know what non-technical people actually need to hear.

When I started building with AI agents a few years ago, those two worlds started collapsing into each other.

Each generation of models got me further. What used to take weeks started taking days. Then hours. But the models that came out in December were a different thing entirely.

I described what I wanted. An AI agent built it. I shipped a product with auth, payments, and a real database. In a weekend.

Since then I've been building everything this way. AI agents for growth, for content, for SEO. Entire businesses run on workflows I never could have built alone.

And I keep thinking: there are so many people who could do this too. They just don't know where to start.

So I'm building a course. It's an experiment and a work in progress, and I'm figuring it out as I go.

It's called The Builder Generalist (buildergeneralist.com). It takes you from "I've never opened a terminal" to "I just shipped and deployed a real product." Using AI agents.

What's inside:

- How to install and use Claude Code (step by step, no assumptions about what you already know)
- How to validate an idea using AI for market research
- How to guide AI agents to build features, design interfaces, and write code
- How to deploy, launch, and get your first users
- How to build your own AI agents (using the ones I built as templates)

First cohort is starting soon. It's async, and Module 0 is free so you can try it before deciding.

I believe specialization is becoming less important. The people who thrive will be the ones who can do a little bit of everything: strategy, design, product, growth. And use AI to close the gaps between what they know and what they need.

Builder generalists. That's the bet.
